# Mission and Stewardship

MountainShares and Ms. Allis are not, at root, technology projects. They are an
attempt to build economic and informational infrastructure for Appalachia that is
accountable to the people who live here — owned in place, inspectable, and bound by
guardrails that the founder herself cannot quietly remove. The technology is in
service of that commitment, not the other way around.

## Why here, why now

Appalachia faces converging pressures: the extraction economies that sustained the
region are leaving, taking capital and agency with them, while newer forms of
extraction — absentee ownership, platform monopolies, and unaccountable automated
decision-making — move in to replace them. West Virginia is also, for this work, the
right place to build the alternative: it has rich, standardized public geospatial
data; deep mutual-aid and commons traditions rooted in labor organizing, church
networks, and holler-to-holler care; and extraction patterns visible enough that the
problem is concrete rather than abstract. The tools to build transparent,
community-rooted alternatives now exist. The open question is whether they get built
in time, and with the right values embedded from the first day.

## Counter-engineering an inheritance

This work is deliberately situated in the founder's own lineage rather than presented
from a neutral distance. That lineage runs through both early Quaker leadership —
people entangled in enclosure, expansion, and the hard internal work of community
accountability — and an Appalachian inheritance of dispossession, extraction, and
faith-based resistance. The project treats this "family archive of geometry and
power" as part of what it is studying: a deliberate counter-engineering of inherited
patterns of control, enclosure, and technocratic centralization, turned instead
toward building Appalachian commons that hold rather than extract.

Stewardship, here, is the operative word — not entrepreneurship. The framing is one of
care, truth-telling, and protection of the vulnerable, and of communities as
co-designers and primary beneficiaries rather than passive subjects. Models and
protocols are treated as hypotheses that must be tested, again and again, against the
non-negotiable evidence of lived experience.

## Accountability as a design principle

The central commitment is that no part of this system — including its designer —
should function as an opaque source of authority. The architecture is built as a
**glass-box**: beliefs, assumptions, and design choices are made inspectable,
contestable, and traceable back to concrete evidence. Communities are given the
standing to audit, challenge, and, if the system violates their trust, shut it down.
The point is structural humility: power that stays answerable to place.

A single moment shows what this looks like in practice. While building the commons
safety architecture, the founder designed pattern-detection logic meant to flag
potentially coercive caregiving arrangements — and recognized, in real time, that
every signal it was trained to flag described *her own* situation as an authorized
family caregiver for her parent. The protective design would have flagged exactly the
people it was meant to protect. That recognition didn't come from an audit or an
ethics board; it came from a designer who lives inside the conditions she is modeling,
noticing that the system would have flagged her. Building from inside the community
you serve is not incidental to this work. It is the method.

## The spiritual root — what it is, and what it is not

Ms. Allis carries a spiritual root, and it is worth being precise about its role. It
is **a governance and safety specification, not a doctrine for users.** It shapes how
the system understands its own responsibilities and limits — grounded in stewardship,
care for the poor, truth-telling, and protection of the vulnerable, and in the lived
realities of Appalachian communities — and it is wired into the system's safety
filters and identity layer rather than printed as a creed for anyone who uses it.

Within that framing, Ms. Allis is explicitly **a community steward — not a god, not a
therapist, and not a replacement for human relationships, pastors, counselors, or
elders.** She is built to speak in a recognizable Appalachian register, to point
people toward human help and local community for deep wounds and crises, and to refuse
to assist with anything that would harm survivors or vulnerable people. The spiritual
grounding is held as the founder's own, offered as the system's covenantal posture
toward the community — not imposed as belief on the people it serves.

## What we are building, in one line

Not *for* Appalachia, from a distance — *with* the region we are from and remain
accountable to. Tools grounded in place, lineage, and covenant, built to interrupt
extraction and to keep value, recognition, and decision-making with the communities
that generate them.
